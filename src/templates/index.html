Act I · The Myth

The Myth of the Meteorite Magnet

From a scientific perspective we can absolutely assume that meteorites have fallen completely evenly across every square kilometer of Earth throughout its history.

Earth is a moving target. It does not stand still but rotates on its axis and races around the sun.

A square kilometer of ocean or desert or a small town all have the exact same statistical probability of being hit.

What we see on the map here are not the places where meteorites fall but rather where they are found.

That is a massive difference.

Whether a meteorite falls is a question of physics, and physics is the same everywhere on Earth. Whether a meteorite is found is a question of people, and people are spread very unevenly across the planet.

Act II · The Human Factor

The Human Factor

The moment you overlay the find map with two deeply human variables, population density and gross domestic product per capita, the pattern becomes startlingly readable. The brightest regions on the find map are almost identical to the places where many people live or where serious money flows into science.

Two closely intertwined reasons are at work here.

Population density

Eyes on the ground

Why does population matter? The more people live or farm in an area the more eyes are directed at the ground. Many finds are purely accidental discoveries by farmers or walkers.

Wealth · GDP per capita

Money for the search

Why does wealth and GDP matter? Rich countries have the budget for research and camera systems that monitor the sky as well as targeted expeditions.

Together these two factors create a massive wealth bias in the data. A fallen meteorite in a Norwegian field finds its way into a museum register within weeks. An identical meteorite that lands at the same second somewhere in the Congolese jungle is most likely lost forever, unnoticed, uncharted, uncounted.

In the end the map does not show where meteorites are common. It shows where we bother to look.

Act III · The Treasure Map

The Meteorite Treasure Map: Where the next big discovery awaits

From the logic of the previous two acts the conclusion follows almost inevitably. We now know that meteorites fall evenly everywhere. So if we want to calculate where the most valuable undiscovered meteorites lie we must combine two factors: maximum preservation meets minimal searching. That is where the greatest unrecovered treasure of science lies.

You can experiment on the map above yourself. Toggle the layers for NASA finds and global aridity on and off. The golden points that remain are not random hits but the result of a precise prediction formula.

Criterion 1 · Preservation

Aridity Index < 0.1

These are true extreme deserts. The annual rainfall is far below the potential evaporation. Whether in the Sahara or the Atacama or the Iranian highlands iron nickel meteorites survive here for millennia without rusting away. Whatever falls from the sky here stays intact on the ground.

Criterion 2 · The Research Gap

Find Density < 0.8

Here is where the wheat is separated from the chaff. The map rigorously filters out all areas that have already been successfully searched. This leads to an astonishing effect: known meteorite hotspots like certain regions in the USA or familiar search paths in Morocco do NOT light up here. Why? Because countless collectors and researchers have already been there. The probability of finding a completely new and obvious meteorite there today drops massively because the easy finds have long been picked up.

The Paradox of the North: If you look at North Africa on the map the prediction becomes perfectly visible. While the edges of the Sahara where many people live or can easily travel are already well searched the deep inaccessible Sahara like in southern Libya or Algeria literally lights up. The physical conditions there are exactly the same as at the found locations. Physics tells us that a meteorite that struck this golden area 5000 years ago is highly likely still lying there today visible like a misprint on white paper. What is missing in these glowing zones is not extraterrestrial material. It is simply a journey there.

Anyone who wants to make the next big discovery must travel exactly to where this image is glowing right now.

The Western Sahara or the inner Iran Lut desert or the Atacama plateau or parts of Inner Mongolia. These marked zones are the treasure chests of the coming decades. They hold the highest probability for previously undiscovered meteorite classes that will directly shape our understanding of the early solar system.

The fact that this map can be read so clearly is the most beautiful confirmation of our initial thesis: meteorites fall completely evenly everywhere. We just do not see them where nobody looks.
